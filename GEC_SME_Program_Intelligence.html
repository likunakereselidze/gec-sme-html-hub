Ideal Customer Profiles — Conversion Playbooks

ICP #1 — Tier 2 · High Conversion

The Self-Made Ceiling Hitter

"I built this from nothing — but something broke and I can't see what"

The Company

  • Revenue: GEL 3–7M
  • Employees: 25–80
  • Age: 5–12 years in operation
  • Sectors: Manufacturing, distribution, import/wholesale, FMCGi production, construction materials
  • Growth trajectory: Grew fast for 3–5 years, now flat or growing slower than costs
  • Structure: Founder is the bottleneck. No real middle management.

The Person

  • Role: Founder & Owner (100% or majority)
  • Age: 38–50
  • Education: University degree, no MBAi or formal management training
  • Identity: Self-made. Built this from zero. Deep industry knowledge, learned by doing
  • Decision style: Gut-feel, fast, centralized. Delegates tasks, not authority

Psychology & Pain Points

  • Core pain: "Everything still runs through me." Can't take a two-week vacation.
  • Hidden fear: Business can't survive without him — burning out
  • Status anxiety: Sees peers growing faster. Wonders what they know.
  • Relationship to learning: Skeptical of classroom settings. Respects practical knowledge only

Objection Sequence

1st: "I don't have time" 2nd: "I know my business" 3rd: "Who are these mentors?" 4th: "Is this another training?"
"I don't have time" means: "I'm scared that if I step away for 4 hours a week, things will fall apart — which proves the exact problem this program solves."

Entry Message & Channel

"We're selecting 20 companies for the founding cohort. Your revenue proves you know how to grow a business. The question is whether you've built one that can grow without you in the room."
  • Primary channel: Bank RMi warm intro
  • Secondary: Direct outreach from GEC (personal letter, not mass email)
  • Positioning door: Prestige ("We Choose You")

How They See Themselves as Owner / CEO

"I am the business. Every deal, every client, every important call runs through me. That's not ego — that's how it works. People depend on me. If I stop, everything stops. I'm not a manager, I'm a machine."

They have not made the identity shift from operator to owner. They measure themselves by how busy they are, not by what the business produces without them. The program needs to reframe their identity — not attack it.

What They've Already Tried

Enterprise Georgia programs — attended 1–2, useful but too generic and free. Didn't feel like their peers were in the room.
Hired a "strategic advisor" — consultant delivered recommendations they didn't implement. "Got a beautiful document. Nothing changed."
Sent employees to trainings — paid for a manager to attend a course. Behavior didn't change. "Waste of time."
Business books / podcasts — read E-Myth, Scaling Up, maybe Traction. Agreed with everything. Applied nothing.
Their conclusion: "None of it worked because nobody was IN my business with me." This is exactly the program's differentiator.

Conversion Trigger

Someone they know and respect — a peer at their revenue level — is already in or joining the program. Not an advertisement. Not a presentation. A real person from their world saying: "I'm going. You should too." FOMOi closes this profile, not logic. Bank RM who has a personal relationship (not just business) is the second-best trigger.

ICP #2 — Tier 2 · High Conversion with Trigger

The Legacy Modernizer

"My family built something real — I need to make it survive the next 20 years"

The Company

  • Revenue: GEL 5–10M
  • Employees: 40–150
  • Age: 10–25 years (often started by parent/uncle)
  • Sectors: Agro-processing, retail chain, food production, wine/beverages, pharmacy chain
  • Growth trajectory: Stable but stagnant. Revenue maintained through relationships, not systems

The Person

  • Role: Owner (inherited or co-owns with family) or family-appointed CEO
  • Age: 30–45
  • Education: Often studied abroad (Turkey, EU, US) or at top Georgian university. May have MBAi
  • Decision style: More analytical, constrained by family dynamics
  • Network: Broader — alumni networks, business events, some international exposure

Psychology & Pain Points

  • Core pain: Business is fragile — depends on 3 old relationships, 30% revenue from one client
  • Hidden fear: Modernizing will break what works — family will blame them
  • Ambition: Export, EU standards, attract outside investment or strategic partner

Objection Sequence

1st: "Who are these mentors?" 2nd: "I've seen programs like this" 3rd: "Is this calibrated for my level?"
"Who are these mentors?" means: "I need to know this room won't be full of people who are less experienced than me. I'm looking up, not down."

Entry Message & Channel

"Your family built something that survived the hardest years of Georgian business. The question now isn't survival — it's whether the next chapter is growth by design or drift by default."
  • Primary channel: Personal invitation from GEC leadership or consortium partner
  • Secondary: LinkedIn, industry events
  • Positioning door: Prestige + Peer Community hybrid

How They See Themselves as Owner / CEO

"I'm the steward of something bigger than me. I have more education than whoever built this, but modernizing feels like rewriting a history I didn't write. The family watches everything. If I change something and it breaks, it's on me. If I don't change it, we slowly die."

Identity is split between the innovator they want to be and the guardian the family needs. They use sophisticated language (strategy, positioning, culture) but operate under family approval constraints. They compare themselves to international standards, not local peers.

What They've Already Tried

Executive MBA or courses abroad — Caucasus School of Business, Free University, possibly a summer school in Europe/Turkey. Has the theoretical base. Execution is the gap.
Hired McKinsey/Deloitte equivalent — or a senior consultant. Got a 60-slide strategy deck. "It sat on a shelf. My team didn't understand it, and honestly neither did I after 3 months."
Forbes Georgia, TBCi/BoGi events — attends regularly. Good for network. Learning value is low — same people, same topics every year.
LinkedIn Learning / Coursera — started multiple courses. Rarely finishes. "Too generic, not Georgia-specific, can't apply it here."
Their gap: theory is fine. The program that works with their specific Georgian-market business, with peers at their level, is what they haven't found. They're looking up, not around.

Conversion Trigger

They see the cohort composition — specifically, who the other 9 companies are. Social calibration is the trigger: "Are these people at my level or above? Would I be embarrassed to be the least-developed business in the room?" If the answer is no — if the room feels like peers or aspirational peers — they convert. The mentor roster (Forbes 30u30i, EBRDi, Deloitte, World Bank backgrounds) also moves them when they see it in full.

ICP #3 — Tier 3 · Possible with Peer Referral

The Burned Pragmatist

"Show me the numbers or don't waste my time"

The Company

  • Revenue: GEL 2–5M
  • Employees: 15–50
  • Age: 6–15 years
  • Sectors: IT services, construction, import/distribution, e-commerce, logistics
  • Growth trajectory: Plateaued. Revenue hasn't moved meaningfully in 2–3 years

The Person

  • Role: Founder & Owner, or CEO with equity
  • Age: 40–55
  • Identity: Pragmatic, data-oriented, slightly cynical. Values results over credentials
  • Decision style: Slow, evidence-based. Will research before committing. Asks for references
  • Network: Small but deep. 5–6 trusted people whose opinions matter

Psychology & Pain Points

  • Core pain: Has tried consultants, trainings, even hired a "strategic advisor." Got PowerPoints. Nothing changed.
  • Hidden fear: That the problem might actually be him
  • Trust mechanism: Peer validation only. Advertising won't reach him.

Objection Sequence

1st: "I've seen programs like this" 2nd: "1,000/month for what?" 3rd: "I don't have time" 4th: "Show me someone it worked for"
"I've seen programs like this" means: "I've been disappointed before. Prove this is different."

Entry Message & Channel

"You've been pitched before. Consultants with frameworks, workshops with certificates. Here's what we do differently: 20 companies, 13 mentors who've built businesses themselves, one year of weekly work on your actual problems. No theory."
  • Primary channel: Warm referral from a trusted peer
  • Secondary: Bank RM who knows them personally
  • Positioning door: ROI Math ("The Math Is Obvious")

How They See Themselves as Owner / CEO

"I've earned my skepticism. I've been at this long enough to know when someone is selling me something they can't deliver. I know my market, I know my margins, I know exactly where the inefficiencies are. What I need is implementation, not another framework. Talk is cheap."

They see themselves as an experienced, data-driven executive. They define themselves by their track record and their practical knowledge. They don't admit weakness easily — especially in front of people they perceive as less experienced. The program's threat: being the smartest person in a room of 9 other confused owners.

What They've Already Tried — and Why It Burned Them

2–3 business coaches — paid individually. "Smart people. Good conversations. Then I left and nothing was different 3 months later."
EU4Businessi / Enterprise Georgia — multiple programs. "Fine for people who are just starting. I need something for where I am now."
OKRi or KPIi workshops — tried to implement. Lasted 2–3 weeks. Team didn't buy in. Owner didn't have time to maintain it. "We went back to how things were."
Hired a consultant for strategy — got a 70-slide deck. Paid GEL 8,000–15,000. "It was right about everything and useful for nothing."
Every failure builds the wall higher. Each disappointment increases the proof threshold for the next offer. Do NOT try to sell past this wall — go around it with peer validation.

Conversion Trigger

Only one thing works: a real business owner they respect (not a friend — a peer at their revenue level they consider sharp) personally tells them it's worth it. Not a testimonial. Not a brochure. A direct conversation: "I'm in Cohort 1. You should call them." Cannot be sold, only referred. Implication: in Cohort 1, recruit a Burned Pragmatist only if you have a Tier 1 or Tier 2 participant who knows them. Otherwise, this profile belongs to Cohort 2.

ICP #4 — Tier 1 · Highest Conversion

The Exit Architect

"I'm not running a business — I'm building an asset. I need it to be worth something to someone else."

The Company

  • Revenue: GEL 4–10M
  • Employees: 20–80
  • Age: 8–20 years
  • Sectors: Manufacturing, logistics, F&B, pharma, IT services, retail chain — any sector with trade value
  • Growth trajectory: Healthy or plateauing — the goal isn't more revenue, it's higher valuation multiplei
  • Structure: May have brought in a key manager or co-owner. Has thought about what happens if they step back.

The Person

  • Role: Founder and majority owner, or CEO with significant equityi
  • Age: 40–55
  • Education: Often formal — MBA or executive education. Has consumed investment/finance content independently
  • Decision style: Strategic, multi-year horizon. Makes decisions by calculating what adds vs. removes from the business's sale value
  • Network: Has contacts in banking and investment circles. Has attended Axel, TBCi Capital, BoGi Capital events

Psychology & Pain Points

  • Core pain: Business is too owner-dependent to attract serious buyers or investors. Valuation multiple is low because "the asset is you."
  • Hidden fear: "If I leave, the business falls apart — which means it's not really an asset, it's just a job with extra steps."
  • Ambition: Sell equity stake, attract a strategic or financial partner, or full exiti within 3–5 years
  • Financial sophistication: Understands EBITDAi, DCFi, multiples conceptually — but hasn't applied them to their own business yet
  • External signal: Had 1–2 exploratory conversations with banks or investors. Was told "not ready" or offered a low valuation they rejected.

Objection Sequence

1st: "How is this different from hiring a consultant?" 2nd: "Has anyone in your program actually raised or sold?" 3rd: "Is this calibrated for where I am?"
"Has anyone actually raised money?" means: "I'm not paying to learn theory. Show me this creates a real transaction outcome, not just a better PowerPoint."

Entry Message & Channel

"You've built something with real value. The question is whether it's structured so that someone else can see that value — and act on it. The program's final session is a Growth Plan presentation in front of banks and potential investors. That's not an exercise. That's your first real step toward a transaction."
  • Primary channel: Bank private banking / TBCi Capital / BoGi Capital RMi
  • Secondary: Axel angel network events, direct GEC invitation
  • Positioning door: ROI Math — but the ROI is an exit multiple, not just learning

How They See Themselves as Owner / CEO

"I'm not just running a business — I'm building an asset. My operational involvement is temporary. My ownership stake is the real product. I think in multiples and exit scenarios. I need the company to run without me so I can either sell it or attract capital."

Most aligned with the program's investment readiness arc. They already think like an investor. They just need the execution framework and the platform (Week 40 investor demo) to make it real. This profile has the clearest ROI calculation of all 5 — and they've already done the math.

What They've Already Tried

Exploratory meetings with banks/investors — went to TBC Capital, BoG Capital, possibly Axel. Was told "come back when financials are cleaner" or offered a multiple they rejected.
Business valuation exercise — may have had an informal valuation done. Didn't like the number. Understood why but didn't know how to change it.
Financial reporting software / accounting — uses 1C, possibly Excel-based management accounting. Inconsistent. Investor-grade financials don't exist yet.
Followed investment content — reads The Economist, Forbes Georgia, maybe TBC's business content. Has language but not the implementation playbook.
They know exactly what they need. They've been looking for the right structured vehicle to get there. This program is that vehicle — if you show them Module 15 (Investment Readiness) and the Week 40 investor demo day.

Conversion Trigger — Tier 1

Show them the specific program output: Module 15 — Investment Readiness, and Week 40 — Growth Plan presented to banks and investors. This is not a graduation ceremony — it's a real event with real financial institutions in the room. That single detail changes the conversation from "interesting program" to "I need to be there." Combine with: bank RM intro who already knows their exit ambition.

ICP #5 — Tier 1 · Highest Conversion

The People-Crisis Owner

"I'm brilliant at what we do. Managing people is a completely different game — and it's breaking my business."

The Company

  • Revenue: GEL 2–7M
  • Employees: 25–80 (just crossed a management inflection point — too big to manage personally, too small for a real HRi department)
  • Age: 4–15 years
  • Sectors: Hospitality, retail chains, construction, manufacturing, services — sectors where the product quality depends on people, not just systems
  • Symptom: High turnover, low team performance without direct supervision, failed KPIi attempts, "key person" dependence below owner level

The Person

  • Role: Founder/Owner — became a manager by default, not by design
  • Age: 35–48
  • Background: Domain expert — engineer, chef, construction professional, logistics specialist. Built the business on technical excellence, not management skills
  • Decision style: Fast on technical and operational decisions. Paralyzed on people decisions — avoids difficult conversations, tolerates low performance too long
  • Self-awareness: High — they KNOW people management is their gap. Not defensive about it. That makes them easier to reach.

Psychology & Pain Points

  • Core pain: High turnover costs money and kills momentum. Team underperforms without the owner present. Owner can't scale because they're still the best manager in the room.
  • Hidden fear: "If I build real management systems, I'll lose control of quality. The things that made us good are informal — what if formalizing kills the culture?"
  • Active crisis: Has a recent trigger — a key employee just quit, a management conflict broke a project, or a second location is failing without them there
  • Specific need: Leadership identity work, HR frameworks, delegation structure — Modules 4 and 5 directly

Objection Sequence

1st: "This is about business strategy — my problem is people" 2nd: "I don't have time" 3rd: "Will this be generic or specific to my situation?"
"My problem is people" means: "I'm looking for something that directly addresses HR and leadership — not a strategy program where HR is one slide." Answer: 7 dedicated sessions on leadership and HR (Modules 4 and 5), plus individual mentoring with Ia Tsulaia (100+ OD projects).

Entry Message & Channel

"Every founder is brilliant at the thing they built the business around. Very few are born managers. The program has a dedicated 7-session block on leadership and human capital — built specifically for owner-operators who are in over their heads with people. It's led by an expert with 20+ years and 100+ organizational transformation projects. This isn't a workshop. It's a working session on your actual team."
  • Primary channel: Bank RM (especially hospitality/retail segment), peer referral from someone who solved same problem
  • Secondary: Direct GEC outreach if company fits sector profile
  • Positioning door: Community + specific module relevance ("A Room That Gets You")

How They See Themselves as Owner / CEO

"I know my product better than anyone on earth. I can solve any technical problem in this business in 10 minutes. But people — I became the boss by default, not by choice. I'm a professional first, a manager second. And I don't like that second part."

Unlike most ICPs, this profile is NOT ego-driven about their management gap. They're self-aware and somewhat relieved when someone acknowledges the problem clearly. They respond to specificity and credibility — not inspiration, not theory, not yet another generic HR framework. Ia Tsulaia's 100+ projects and government-level track record is a direct trust signal for this profile.

What They've Already Tried

Hired an HR manager — focused on paperwork and contracts. Culture didn't change. "I needed a coach, not an administrator."
HR / management workshops — attended 1–2 generic sessions (Enterprise Georgia level). "Not relevant to my team size or situation."
Tried KPIs / performance systems — rolled out, team saw it as control, not development. Abandoned after a few months.
Team-building events — dinners, trips, activities. Good short-term effect. "Back to normal in 2 weeks."
They haven't found a program that works WITH them on their specific team, not around generic HR principles. That's exactly what Ia Tsulaia's module structure is designed to deliver.

Conversion Trigger — Tier 1

The crisis moment IS the conversion moment. When a key employee quits, when a second location fails, when the owner works a 14-hour day because nobody else can — that's the window. The trigger message: show them Ia Tsulaia's full profile (100+ projects, 20+ years, GIZi/EBRDi/UNDPi/NATOi track record) and Modules 4–5 in detail. Then ask: "Has anything you've tried so far actually changed how your team operates?" The silence is the conversion.

What "Coachable" Actually Means
Coachability is not personality. It's not education level. It's not how polished they present. It is one question: will this person actually change their behavior based on external input?

The peer cohort format requires everyone to contribute honestly and receive challenge without shutting down. One non-coachable person doesn't just harm themselves — they degrade the experience for all 9 others. The gate is brand protection, not gatekeeping.
Why Objectivity Matters
In Georgian business culture, rejection without a rubric becomes personal — "GEC didn't take me" vs. "I scored 14/30 and need 6 more months of readiness." A documented scoring system:

→ Protects GEC from relationship damage
→ Gives rejected applicants a clear path back
→ Creates a consistent standard across all interviewers
→ Defends decisions internally when someone "important" applies

Top 3 Critical Risks — Expanded Playbook
Critical Risk #1
Wrong Cohort Composition — the Program Killer
How It Happens
Pressure to fill 20 spots leads to compromising on coachability gate. Or: someone "important" applies and the gate isn't enforced for social/political reasons. One wrong member degrades peer dynamics, reduces session quality, and creates the first negative story about the program.
Early Warning Signs
Applicant deflects during interview, blames others for business problems, asks "what will I get" before "what is expected of me," sends someone else to the interview, pushes back on the NDA.
Mitigation Protocol
Score using the coachability rubric before discussing enrollment terms. Two-person interview minimum. If score is <18, offer Module 1 trial only. Never fill a spot from pressure. 9 right people is better than 10 wrong ones.
Critical Risk #2
Mentor Non-Attendance — the Trust Destroyer
How It Happens
Mentors are volunteers or low-fee contributors with their own businesses. A busy season, travel, or simply losing interest means sessions get cancelled last-minute. Participants who paid GEL 12,000 expect to see the people they were sold.
Early Warning Signs
Mentor misses planning meeting. Delays confirming session dates. Delegates prep work to assistant. Hasn't responded to coordinator in 10+ days. "I'll confirm next week" repeated multiple times.
Mitigation Protocol
Signed commitment document (not verbal). 48-hour notice minimum for changes. Each module has a named backup mentor. Coordinator does a confirmation call 7 days and 2 days before each session. Any mentor who misses 2 sessions without prior notice is replaced.
Critical Risk #3
Confidentiality Breach — the Permanent Damage
How It Happens
Tbilisi is a small business world. A participant casually mentions "Company X told us their margins are only 8%" at a dinner. It travels back. In Georgian business culture, this kind of breach is career-ending for the program — not just for the individual.
Early Warning Signs
Session content shared in WhatsApp groups. Participant posts about another's business on social media. Second-hand information from the cohort appears in a competitor conversation.
Mitigation Protocol
NDA signed before first session — not at enrollment. Chatham House Rulei announced at the start of every single session. Explicit "no screenshots, no names outside this room" norm. First breach: private warning + written reminder. Second breach: exit from program, no refund, NDA enforcement letter.
